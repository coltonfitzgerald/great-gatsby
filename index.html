The Harlem Renaissance was a cultural takeover in which jazz and other forms of art flourished during this time.

Analyzed Excerpt
“In consequence I’m inclined to reserve all judgments, a habit that has opened up many curious natures to me and also made me the victim of not a few veteran bores. The abnormal mind is quick to detect and attach itself to this quality when it appears in a normal person, and so it came about that in college I was unjustly accused of being a politician, because I was privy to the secret griefs of wild, unknown men. Most of the confidences were unsought—frequently I have feigned sleep, preoccupation, or a hostile levity when I realized by some unmistakable sign that an intimate revelation was quivering on the horizon—for the intimate revelations of young men or at least the terms in which they express them are usually plagiaristic and marred by obvious suppressions.” (Page 3)

Ok, so, this quote (which I’m sure is somewhere near the one everyone picked for this chapter), is fairly difficult to understand at first, but it sure does make a lot of sense. So, in order to look at this quote we have to look before this quote, at his father basically saying “Don’t look down on people because they are poorer/less educated/uglier, etc., than you.” So, when the first sentence starts, Nick is explaining how, because of what his father said to him, he keeps his mouth shut when it comes to judging other people. He then says that this has caused strange or peculiar people (curious natures) to approach him more easily, as they will avoid judgement from him. Another type of person to approach him is the “veteran bore”, someone who is a veteran at being a bore, or boring. Somebody who is really bog-standard and not interesting in the slightest. These people would normally be ridiculed by their colleagues, especially in the roaring 20s. The boring people all worked on Wall Street with other boring people and all the fun people never worked, just partied with other fun people. “The abnormal mind is quick to detect and attach itself to this quality when it appears in a normal person,” The wording on this sentence is a little iffy, if you ask me. Abnormal minds would be those that were stated about 6-7 lines above this one, people that are strange and/or boring. These types of people would immediately recognise that Nick is not the judging type, and would then try to stay around him. He also calls himself normal right at the end of that line, which to me seems very acute. Although, normal isn’t really the word to describe Nick. Something like boring would be better suited. A lot of the action in this story is going to happen around Nick without him ever really being involved. He then goes on to say that he was accused of being a politician, because he knew a lot of secrets belonging to men that he didn’t know. These men also seem to be very in the need for secrets, as they are “wild”, and probably have made their fair share of mistakes. A common stereotype of politicians is the one of a scheming manipulator; someone who will pay or kill for dirt on their competitors to give themselves an edge. Most of these secrets were not confided in him willingly, and he did not necessarily want to know them. Nick learned of these secrets through either pretending to be asleep or busy with other things. The last thing he mentions is “hostile levity”. Levity means something along the lines of “to be joking or jovial but in a rude or disrespecting manner, usually in the face of something serious”. To do this with hostility, Nick would have to basically make a rude joke to get people to shut up about their secrets, at which point they would tell him about their secrets anyways. The last line states that the way that all young men tell their secrets is exactly the same. Their movements and speech patterns are plagiarised, or stolen, from others and they have extremely obvious hesitations when telling them to others.

Historical References
Hydroplanes

During the 1920’s the Hydroplane was just starting to make its first appearances. In Chapter 4, Gatsby brought Nick onto his hydroplane in order to talk and from what it seemed like, gain trust within each other. A hydroplane is a motorboat that is designed to skim over water rather than float in water. They are typically much faster than the average boat.

World Series

Today, America’s favorite sport is easily football. With events like the Superbowl taking over American stores and restaurants as an entire nationwide event, there is no other sport that compares to the popularity of football. In the 1920’s football was nowhere near as popular and America’s favorite sport was baseball. Everyone was into baseball, and the World Series was comparable today to the Superbowl. The World Series was “fixed” by Meyer Wolfsheim and this is linked to an actual event in history called the “Black Box Scandal”

Speakeasy

A speakeasy is a place where bootleggers can buy and sell alcohol and consumers can hang out and drink. Prohibition during this time actually increased the consumption for alcohol on average, and speakeasies during this time were extremely common. The speakeasy mentioned in this chapter was when Nick was introduced to Wolfsheim by Gatsby.

Wolfsheim

Meyer Wolfsheim, a jewish gangster, was introduced to Nick by Gatsby. He was said to have “fixed the World Series” but not much more information was given about this man. Wolfsheim is a representation of a real gangster “Arnold Rothstein” who actually did fix the World Series in real life. Rothstein did this by paying off players of the Chicago Red Sox to lose and bet high amounts of money on the Cincinnati Reds.

Rothstein made roughly $350,000 doing this and event went to trial over the entire event, which was known as “The Black Box Scandal”. Eventually, he was found to not be guilty and was let free.

Historical References
Coupe

The Coupe, mentioned multiple times throughout the Great Gatsby seems to be the only car talked about throughout the novel. A coupe is a 2-door car, popularized around the 1920’s and was the #1 manufactured type of car in this time period. Both Tom and Gatsby own a coupe.

Intermarriage between black and white

Interracial marriage was still a tense subject during this time. Tom, being a gigantic racist, decided to comment on the fact that he believes this is a ridiculous thing to even think of. Tom is racist throughout the book, even noting earlier on that African Americans may take over white people over time.

Bootlegging over the counter drug stores

Bootlegging was a common criminal activity during the 1920’s as alcohol was still under prohibition. During Chapter 7, you will learn that Gatsby was involved in bootlegging alcohol over the counter in drug stores. What this means is that the store clerk would sell alcohol over the counter if asked and Gatsby would make a profit. There are no links in history that say this was a common way of bootlegging alcohol, but it sure is a creative one from F. Scott Fitzgerald. Common places to consume alcohol included homes and speakeasies.

A Speakeasy is a place named after people on the inside “speaking quietly” to conceal the fact the fact that they are consuming alcohol.

Bereavement does funny things to people, mentally. When someone dear to the feelings dies, the brain goes into a state of suspension, almost. Moods drop, obviously, but there can also be other problems. One can start forgetting things they’ve seen or have trouble seeing things at all. Historical References
Funeral Practices

In chapter 9 of The Great Gatsby, Jay Gatsby’s memorial service is being held after he dies at the end of chapter 8. The actual processes of his funeral are not explored in too much depth, because the chapter is mainly people avoiding said funeral and Nick quitting his day job to be a philosopher. During this time period, funerals moved away from in-home services held by family members, instead becoming a profession for others. The National Funeral Directors Association worked to make funerals a tad easier on the families of the recently deceased. Cremation became more common, as spaces to bury bodies became rarer. Funeral ceremonies became more popular (or memorial service, if you were an unlucky bastard), with more than just the immediate family present to celebrate the life and mourn the death of the victim. And, of course, funerals were becoming a paid commodity, and a business venture for those brave enough.

“That’s my middle west—not the wheat or the prairies or the lost Swede towns but the thrilling, returning trains of my youth and the street lamps and sleigh bells in the frosty dark and the shadows of holly wreaths thrown by lighted windows on the snow. I am part of that, a little solemn with the feel of those long winters, a little complacent from growing up in the Carraway house in a city where dwellings are still called through decades by a family’s name. This story makes a fairly large deal over the difference between “New Money” and “Old Money”. By the end of the story, all the new money relationships have ended, Nick and Jordan, Gatsby and Daisy, Tom and Myrtle. But what remains? Old money- Tom and Daisy are back together and moving away. It feels like Fitzgerald has a lot to say about the young, reckless generation, spending their newly-earned money on extravagant parties and shirts. By the end of the story, being a headstrong youngin, blowing his money to get closer to Daisy got Gatsby killed. Nick realises this, that the wealth and power hungry state that New York is in in this time period was very unfitting to all of these Westerners. The East was for boring people who became rich by being from a rich family, and the West was for fools that may have had more spirit, but didn’t have as much wealth or common values. People who lived on the East coast in places like New York looked at the people of the West like Neanderthals, while the people of the West looked on to the people of the East as stuck-up snobs who couldn’t put in an honest day’s work to save their lives. Both sides were generally wrong. But here, the entire main cast of people were so far out of their element that they all either died, moved away, or were unmentioned after they hung up with Nick while he was at work. Even Nick himself moved back to the Midwest because he couldn’t stand living in New York anymore. So, to round off this entire project: “New Money” lived too fast and recklessly in this time period for its own good, just like this time period as a whole did.
